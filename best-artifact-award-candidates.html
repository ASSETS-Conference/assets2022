This year, we have 15 high-quality candidates for the Best Artifact Award. Please check the candidate artifacts and the corresponding submission papers and cast your vote by October 24th.

Please cast your vote considering the following criteria: artifact's potential to improve the quality of life for people with disabilities or older adults; artifact availability for the target user group; artifact's quality level and robustness.

Designing a Game for Pre-Screening Students with Specific Learning Disabilities in Chinese

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

We design and develop a Game for Pre-Screening Students with Specific Learning Disabilities in Chinese. This tool targets students from 5 to 10 years old to screen out whether they are at risk in SLDs. If yes, we will notify the special-ed teachers to facilitate the waiting of assessment. Also, we analyze the strength and weakness of the students. Therefore, special-ed teachers can provide early intervention to those students who are at risk. The artifact online (apple play store) is for demonstration purpose. We offer the full version directly to our beneficiaries, i.e., schools and organizations.

A Collaborative Approach to Support Medication Management in Older Adults with Mild Cognitive Impairment Using Conversational Assistants (CAs)

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

The artifact that we are presenting is called MATCHA - Medication Action To Check-In for Health Application. This artifact is a Google Action, designed using Google's Actions Console. It can be thought of as an extension designed for the Google Home Assistant. This action can be set up to check-in with you at any time(s) during the day. It will begin by asking you whether you have taken your medicine for that time. If you say yes, it applauds you and finishes. If you say no, the assistant asks you if you would like to set a reminder for a later time to be checked-in again for your medications. If you do not remember whether you took the medication or not, the assistant then asks you to go and check your pillbox and confirm whether you did or not. The main feature of this artifact is that it checks-in and not reminds, which is a crucial thing for older adults with early stage dementia, or Mild Cognitive Impairment. More details about the design and testing of this Google Action can be found in our paper. MATCHA can be invoked in any Google Home Smart Home device and in any mobile device that is Android or by downloading the Google Assistant app on iPhones. Once you have access to the Google Assistant, please say "Hey Google, talk to med reminder" and the system will initiate the medication check-in. The process to set up this check-in at specific times in the day can be done through the "Routine" function in the Google Home app available on both Play Store and App Store.

CodeWalk: Facilitating Shared Awareness in Mixed-Ability Collaborative Software Development

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

CodeWalk is a set of features added to Microsoft’s Live Share VS Code extension with support for remote, synchronous code review and refactoring tasks. It uses cursor tethering, speech, and sound effects to make synchronous programming tasks accessible to blind and visually (BVI) impaired developers. Our evaluation of CodeWalk has shown that it streamlines the communication required to refer to source code and enables sighted and BVI developers to spend more time contributing to coding tasks. We have seen almost 700 unique users for CodeWalk since it was made publicly and freely available to all Live Share in November 2021, demonstrating great interest by BVI developers.

Should I Say "Disabled People" or "People with Disabilities"? Language Preferences of Disabled People Between Identity- and Person-First Language

 Description Artifact Link Dataset Link Assets Submission

We created an ongoing global survey to determine the language preferences of disabled people between identity- and person-first language. To date, we have received over 600 responses from over 20 countries that identify their preferences and the reasons for their preferences. We also collect their disability category, age, gender identity, and race to enable researchers to perform intersectional and intrasectional explorations. Our artifact includes a public website that displays the live results from the survey and a public Google Sheet that shows the up-to-date anonymous data from the survey. Through the public website and dataset, researchers and developers can stay up-to-date with the language preferences of disabled people and perform further explorations that will unlock new areas in HCI and Accessibility as well as other domains, such as Disability Studies.

Understanding and Improving Information Extraction From Online Geospatial Data Visualizations for Screen-Reader Users

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

We extended the capabilities of VoxLens, an open-source JavaScript plug-in that makes online data visualizations accessible to screen-reader users using a multi-modal approach, to enable screen-reader users to extract information from geospatial maps. Our code is publicly available via VoxLens' GitHub Repository (specifically, release v.2.1.0). Our artifact is open-source, substantially improves the experiences of screen-reader users with online data visualizations, and reduces the information access equity gap between screen- and non-screen-reader users. Furthermore, it enables developers to improve the functionalities of VoxLens, adding features and optimizations that will enhance the accessibility of complex online data visualizations for screen-reader users.

"What Makes Sonification User-Friendly?" Exploring Usability and User-Friendliness of Sonified Responses

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

We conducted a study with screen-reader users to determine the factors that make the sonification of online data visualizations more user-friendly for screen-reader users. We utilized the findings from our user study to extend the capabilities of Sonifier, an open-source JavaScript plug-in that generates sonified responses from two-dimensional data. Using our code, developers can now generate sonified responses using additional sound types, including MonoSynthesizers and Envelopes, while also modifying configuration options, such as oscillator choices, waveforms, and the number of harmonics to generate the waveform. Our code is publicly available via Sonifier's GitHub Repository (specifically, release v.2.0.0). Our artifact is open-source and improves the experiences of screen-reader users with online data visualizations. Furthermore, it enables developers to improve the functionalities of Sonifier, adding features and optimizations that will enhance the accessibility of complex online data visualizations for screen-reader users.

UnlockedMaps: Visualizing Real-Time Accessibility of Urban Rail Transit Using a Web-Based Map

 Description Artifact Link Stations API Outages API Assets Submission

People who rely on elevators, such as people who use wheelchairs and pregnant people, currently have no map-based tools available to know if there is a real-time elevator outage at a given station. Furthermore, data on elevator outage history is not available to the public. We developed UnlockedMaps, a web-based map that allows users to see in real time how accessible rail transit stations are in six North American cities, including Philadelphia, Chicago, Seattle, Toronto, New York and Bay Area. UnlockedMaps shows which stations are accessible, which stations are not, and which ones are experiencing elevator outages at that given point in time. We collected the elevator outage data from more than 2,300 transit stations over 23 months and released it via a public API. Our artifact contribution includes a: (1) public website that enables users to make informed decisions regarding their commute; and (2) public API that can be used by developers, disability advocates, and policy makers for a variety of purposes, including shedding light on the frequency of elevator outages and their repair times to identify the disparities between neighborhoods in a given city.

Voice-Enabled Blockly: Usability Impressions of a Speech-driven Block-based Programming System

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

This artifact is a speech-driven programming tool. It allows people with upper-body motor impairments to create programs using their voice, in the block-based programming environment Blockly. Using speech, users are able to select, edit, delete, drag and drop elements on the screen. This tool addresses the challenges that people who are not able to move their hands face in block-based programming environments like Blockly. This tool also presents developers with a guide on how to incorporate speech recognition into an existing application. Lastly, it gives accessibility researchers feedback from the evaluation of the prototype in 2 user studies, and design considerations for future voice-based systems based on analyses of user feedback.

AAC with Automated Vocabulary from Photographs: Insights from School and Speech-Language Therapy Settings

 Description Artifact Link (iOS) Artifact Link (Android) Assets Submission

Click AAC is the first AAC application able to generate situation-specific communication boards formed by a combination of descriptive, narrative, and semantic related words and phrases inferred automatically from photographs. The app supports individuals with communication disabilities learning and using symbolic communication and minimizes the burden of pre-programming relevant vocabulary for caregivers and speech language professionals. By providing related vocabulary instantly without requiring users to anticipate the situation, Click AAC can increase the frequency of moments for which the learning of symbols representing real world concepts is possible. In addition, with Click AAC, users do not need to hold in memory the symbols previously selected nor to remember how to navigate to a desired symbol while constructing sentences, reducing memory demands. Finally, the provision of automatic vocabulary can support conversation partners capitalizing on teachable moments by being able to adapt the offered support to emerging and unforeseen situations quickly, and to engage in topics of interest of the learner. Click AAC has been downloaded more than 9000 times, and has appeared in the websites of various organizations specialized in assistive technologies from several countries (TechLab, France; ARASAAC, Spain; Beit Issie Shapiro, Israel), which demonstrates the interest and acceptance of the technology by speech language professionals and their clients with communication disabilities worldwide, despite the prototype nature of the app.

SoundVizVR: Sound Indicators for Accessible Sounds in Virtual Reality for Deaf or Hard of Hearing Users

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

The SoundVizVR Plugin is a sound visualization Unity package for VR projects. With this plugin, designers and developers can bind the sound indicators with a customizable descriptive text or icon to any sound source object in a Unity VR scene, which can rapidly make sounds in the project accessible. This plugin uses textual and iconic representation methods that enable VR users to “see” the existence, loudness, duration, and sound type of the sounds produced by the object.

BentoMuseum: 3D and Layered Interactive Museum Map for Blind Visitors

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

BentoMuseum is a system composed of 3D-printed maps and an iPad application for audio-tactile interactions. It has been used regularly in a science museum called Miraikan, in group visits by special schools for the visually impaired, in public events for the visually impaired, and by requests, to help the users gather information and orient themselves before the visit. Moreover, we open-sourced the data of the 3D models and the iPad application. People can print the models and build the system to learn about the museum without coming to the site. Furthermore, users, developers, and researchers can adapt our method to other places, utilizing our open-source hardware and software.

An Accessible Smart Kitchen Cupboard

 Description Artifact Link Assets Submission

The Smart Cupboard is an innovative intelligent system designed to accommodate the needs of blind individuals, wheelchair users, people with mild cognitive impairments, the elderly, and people without disabilities as well. It is a component of the Intelligent Kitchen and aims to support users in that space by indicating where to find a desired item, assisting in a context-sensitive manner during the cooking process, and enabling the inventory organization process. The cupboard can be opened and closed either by simply touching the surface of its door, or by relevant voice commands, while an embedded touch screen permits the users to get information regarding the contained products. Finally, it is equipped with led lights and a vibration mechanism so as to help the users locate the products that they request.
